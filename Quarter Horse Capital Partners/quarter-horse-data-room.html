Why This Fund · Why Now · Why This Manager

I. We Are the Bank. The SPVs Are the Operators.

Quarter Horse Capital Partners does not own real estate, operate aircraft, mine ore, or hang paintings. The Fund lends money to entities that do those things — and holds senior secured interests in the assets those entities control. Capital flows up through the structure in the form of interest payments, quarterly distributions, and principal repayment. This is the private credit model at its most structurally clean: the Fund is the senior lender, the SPV is the operator, and the collateral is the buffer.

This separation of roles is a feature, not a complexity. It creates liability isolation (a stress event in one SPV does not contaminate the Fund's other positions), operational clarity (each SPV is measured on its own economics), and clean distribution mechanics (cash flows from operations go directly to debt service before any equity distribution). The SPV credit model is how institutional banks, insurance companies, and sovereign lenders have deployed capital into hard assets for decades. Quarter Horse brings this institutional credit architecture to the private fund format.

II. The Yield Gap That Public Markets Cannot Close

The 10-year U.S. Treasury currently yields approximately 4.2%. Investment-grade corporate bonds yield 5–6%. III. Why the SPV Structure Outperforms Direct Asset Ownership

The conventional approach to a multi-asset private fund is direct ownership: the fund buys the real estate, acquires the aircraft, holds the art. This approach concentrates operational risk in the fund manager and creates direct exposure to each asset's operating performance. The SPV lending model inverts this. The Fund holds a credit instrument — a loan with defined terms, defined collateral, and defined default remedies — against an SPV or operator that absorbs the operational complexity.

In a stress scenario, the credit instrument is the recovery mechanism: the Fund enforces its security interest, takes the collateral, and either monetizes it or restructures the facility. The SPV's equity is the first-loss tranche. The Fund's senior secured position is protected by the collateral value. Seven independent SPV collateral pools — each driven by a distinct economic driver — means that only simultaneous, correlated failure across all seven would impair the Fund's ability to meet distribution obligations. Real estate credit stress does not impair precious metals trade receivables. Aviation charter revenue disruption does not affect fine art collateral values. Energy project distress does not impair mineral rights collateral. The SPV architecture creates a cash flow floor that is structurally more resilient than any single-mandate credit vehicle.

Credit Facility Structure: Quarter Horse Capital Partners lends $150M to a Real Estate Special Purpose Vehicle structured to acquire, hold, and operate a diversified portfolio of commercial and residential real estate assets in the United States, with primary concentration in Texas and Sun Belt markets. Capital flows up: rental income, property sale proceeds, and appreciation realizations move through the SPV's waterfall to the Fund as senior debt service before any equity distribution.

SPV Distribution Mechanics: The Real Estate SPV generates income from three sources: (1) rental income from commercial and multifamily tenants; (2) property sales and appreciation realizations; and (3) value-add repositioning exits. This senior secured priority is the defining structural advantage of the SPV lending model versus direct fund ownership of real estate.

Collateral & Security: The Fund holds a first-lien security interest in all real property owned by the SPV, perfected through duly recorded mortgage liens in the applicable county land records. The SPV's real estate portfolio is underwritten against as-is appraised value (not proforma) at conservative LTV ratios — 65–75% on individual assets, with total SPV facility LTV at origination not to exceed 70% of aggregate appraised portfolio value. All mortgages are recorded in the appropriate county land records and are fully enforceable under applicable state law.

Underwriting Criteria: SPV credit facility underwriting requires: (i) independent USPAP-compliant appraisal of all SPV-held assets at origination; (ii) SPV operating history and management team track record review; (iii) market analysis confirming demand for each target property type and submarket; (iv) SPV financial model review including cash flow coverage, debt service coverage ratio, and principal repayment path; (v) legal review of all security instrument enforceability, title chain, and lien priority; and (vi) ongoing covenant compliance requiring maintenance of minimum debt service coverage and maximum LTV across the portfolio.

Exit & Recovery: Primary exit is facility maturity — the SPV refinances with a permanent lender, sells assets from the portfolio, or returns principal from appreciation realizations. Secondary exit: in default, the Fund enforces its first-lien mortgage interests across the SPV's portfolio. Texas's streamlined non-judicial foreclosure process (average 60 days from notice of default to completion) is among the most creditor-friendly in the nation, providing an efficient recovery mechanism for a Texas-concentrated portfolio.

Industrial Sub-Vertical — Mission-Critical Facilities: Within the Real Estate SPV's diversified property mandate, industrial assets include mission-critical facilities, advanced manufacturing plants, reshoring and nearshoring projects, and logistics infrastructure. Institutional-Grade Risk Disclosure — 15 Specific Material Risks

An investment in Quarter Horse Capital Partners involves significant risks, including the possible loss of all principal invested.   • 01

    Risk of Loss of Principal — Including Total Loss

    Investors may lose all or a substantial portion of their invested capital. The Fund invests in illiquid private credit and equity instruments that may decline in value, default, or become worthless. Despite the Fund's diversified collateral architecture, simultaneous impairment across multiple asset classes — while structurally unlikely — cannot be excluded. Investors must be prepared to sustain a complete loss of their investment and should not invest any capital they cannot afford to lose in its entirety.

  • 02

    Illiquidity — Full 3-Year Lockup with No Secondary Market

    Fund interests are illiquid. There is no secondary market for fund membership interests, and none is expected to develop. Investors are subject to a mandatory 3-year lockup during which no redemptions, partial withdrawals, or transfers are permitted without the consent of the managing member. Investors who require access to their capital for any reason during the 3-year term have no mechanism to obtain it. Commit only capital that can be completely unavailable for 36 months.

  • 03

    Key Person Risk — Single Principal Structure

    The Fund's investment strategy, deal sourcing, operator relationships, portfolio management, and day-to-day operations are substantially dependent on Caleb Caraway as the sole investment authority. His death, permanent disability, incapacitation, or voluntary departure would materially impair — and potentially eliminate — the Fund's ability to execute its strategy, maintain portfolio positions, source new deals, and manage existing collateral. The Fund does not have a fully developed succession plan that would replace Mr. Caraway's institutional knowledge across seven lending verticals, his personal business relationships, or his co-investment capital.     Concentration Risk — Large Allocations in Single Asset Classes

    Despite the seven-vertical diversification framework, the Gold/Silver/Commodities, Aviation, and Precious Metals and Structured Private Credit verticals each represent 20% of the $1B base allocation — meaning two of seven verticals (Precious Metals and Aviation) each represent 20% of total capital. Gold and silver have historically experienced sustained corrections of 30–50% lasting multiple years. Oil and gas prices are cyclically volatile and subject to geopolitical, demand, and structural factors outside the Fund's control.

Regulation D — Rule 506(c): The Offering Framework

Rule 506(c) of Regulation D, promulgated under Section 4(a)(2) of the Securities Act of 1933, is the federal securities law exemption under which Quarter Horse Capital Partners offers its membership interests. Key features:

  • No registration required: Fund interests are not registered with the SEC or any state securities authority — the 506(c) exemption permits the offering without registration
  • General solicitation permitted: Unlike Rule 506(b), Rule 506(c) permits general advertising and solicitation — but all purchasers must be independently verified as Accredited Investors
  • Mandatory verification: Self-certification by the investor is insufficient under Rule 506(c). The issuer must take "reasonable steps to verify" accredited investor status through independent means — tax returns, brokerage statements, CPA or attorney letter, etc. SCALE LLP supervises all verification
  • Unlimited capital raising: No cap on the amount raised or number of accredited investors
  • Form D filing: A Form D notice must be filed with the SEC within 15 days of first sale; publicly available on EDGAR
  • Restricted securities: Fund interests are "restricted securities" under Rule 144 — no resale without registration or applicable exemption

Section 3(c)(1): Investment Company Act Exclusion

The Fund relies on Section 3(c)(1) of the Investment Company Act of 1940, which excludes from the definition of "investment company" any issuer:

  • Whose outstanding securities are beneficially owned by not more than 100 persons (the "100-investor cap")
  • That is not making or proposing to make a public offering of its securities

Key implications for investors: (i) the Fund is not required to register as an investment company — no Investment Company Act investor protections apply; (ii) the 100-investor cap is a hard statutory limit — the Fund cannot accept the 101st investor without restructuring or registration; (iii) each investor (regardless of commitment size) counts as one beneficial owner for §3(c)(1) purposes; (iv) early sovereign investors have a structural advantage — a $1B sovereign commitment uses only one of the 100 available slots while accessing the full 17% return tier.

Precious Metals — LBMA Good Delivery Standard

All physical gold and silver transacted through the Fund's trade finance program adheres to the London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) Good Delivery standard — the globally recognized benchmark for responsible sourcing of precious metals. LBMA Good Delivery requires:

  • Metal sourced only from LBMA-accredited refiners with verified responsible sourcing credentials
  • Full chain-of-custody documentation from mine to refined product to delivery
  • Conflict-mineral compliance — no gold sourced from conflict-affected or high-risk areas (CAHRA) as defined by the OECD Due Diligence Guidance
  • Seller identity verification (KYC/AML) for all counterparties in the trade finance cycle
  • DMCC compliance for UAE-based transactions, which requires LBMA-aligned sourcing standards for all physical metal

Institutional note: Sovereign wealth funds with responsible investment mandates should confirm with the Fund's operations team that the specific sourcing channels used in the UAE trade finance program meet their internal ESG screening criteria.
