Section 02
Executive Summary
The case for U.S. critical infrastructure senior secured credit at institutional scale
The Opportunity in One Sentence: Backbone Infrastructure Credit Fund I, LP deploys institutional capital into first-lien senior secured loans against three categories of mission-critical U.S. infrastructure — data centers, energy transition assets, and industrial real property — generating 14–17% net IRR through yield and structured equity upside, with hard asset collateral backing every dollar deployed, and complete IRC §892 tax exemption for sovereign wealth fund investors.

The Market Gap We Fill

The U.S. middle-market infrastructure financing gap has widened dramatically since Basel III and Basel IV capital rules took effect, constraining bank lending to $20M–$150M infrastructure transactions. These deals are:

  • Too large for community banks and regional lenders
  • Too small for direct SWF bilateral deployment
  • Too complex for traditional credit funds with rigid mandates
  • Too essential to remain unfinanced — demand is structural

Backbone fills this gap with a dedicated, operationally focused lending platform built around three highly complementary asset verticals that share deal sourcing infrastructure, underwriting methodology, and collateral enforcement protocols.

Why SWFs Cannot Replicate This Internally

Sovereign wealth funds face structural barriers to accessing this yield stratum directly:

  • Deal sourcing: Middle-market relationships require permanent local origination infrastructure — not deployable at SWF scale
  • Deal sizing: A $5B SWF infrastructure allocation cannot efficiently underwrite 80 × $40M loans
  • Operational overhead: Asset management, workout, and enforcement require specialized local teams
  • Regulatory compliance: U.S. collateral perfection, UCC filings, and enforcement require specialized legal infrastructure

Backbone provides turnkey access to a yield profile that is otherwise structurally inaccessible to SWF capital at scale.

Section 03
Investment Thesis
Macro catalysts, structural dislocations, and why the moment for this strategy is now
Core Thesis: The United States is undergoing the most capital-intensive infrastructure transformation in its history. Data sovereignty, energy transition, and industrial reshoring are simultaneously demanding trillions in new hard-asset infrastructure — while the banking system's capacity to finance middle-market infrastructure projects has been systematically reduced by regulation. The result is a persistent, structural yield premium for non-bank lenders with the expertise to underwrite complex collateral at speed. Backbone Infrastructure Credit Fund I is built to systematically harvest this premium on behalf of institutional capital.
🏗️
The $3 Trillion Infrastructure Financing Gap
The American Society of Civil Engineers estimates $3.0T in infrastructure investment is required over the next decade. Federal funding (IIJA, IRA) covers roughly $1.2T. The remaining $1.8T must come from private capital — creating an unprecedented private credit opportunity in essential infrastructure assets with hard collateral, predictable cash flows, and essential-service demand characteristics.
🤖
The AI Compute Buildout — $500B+ in Data Center Capital Needs
Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) have committed over $500B in U.S. data center buildout through 2028. Developer-side financing for the projects feeding these hyperscaler contracts — colocation operators, specialized facility developers, edge computing networks — creates enormous demand for project-level debt at 10–14% rates, secured by contracted revenue streams and hard real property.
⚡
Energy Transition Creates Hard-Asset Lending Opportunities
The U.S. power grid requires $4.5T in new generation and transmission capacity through 2050. Peaker plants, grid-scale battery storage, solar+storage hybrid facilities, and transmission infrastructure are being financed today at 9–13% spreads, backed by 15–25 year power purchase agreements with investment-grade counterparties — the definition of hard-asset, contracted-cash-flow collateral.
🏭
Industrial Reshoring — $800B in New U.S. Manufacturing
CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act, and executive order-driven reshoring have committed $800B+ in new U.S. industrial facilities — semiconductor fabs, EV battery plants, defense manufacturing, pharmaceutical manufacturing. This wave of mission-critical industrial real property creates first-lien mortgage lending opportunities at 55–60% LTV against assets that cannot be relocated or replaced without enormous cost.
🏦
Bank Withdrawal Widens the Non-Bank Premium
Basel III Endgame rules, DFAST stress testing, and CRE concentration limits have caused U.S. banks to reduce infrastructure lending by 18% since 2022. Regional banks — historically the primary source of $20M–$100M infrastructure loans — have become systematically constrained. This regulatory-driven withdrawal has widened non-bank lending spreads by 200–350 bps and shows no signs of reversing under current capital rules.
🌐
SWF Strategic Imperative: U.S. Hard Asset Exposure
Global sovereign wealth funds have collectively identified U.S. infrastructure as a core strategic allocation — but lack efficient vehicles for middle-market deployment. Direct bilateral deals require local infrastructure, legal teams, and origination relationships that cannot be built cost-effectively for sub-$200M exposures. Backbone provides single-ticket access to a diversified book of 30–50 U.S. infrastructure loans — one commitment, institutional-grade management, quarterly cash returns.

Why Now — The Timing Is Structural, Not Cyclical

The investment opportunity in U.S. infrastructure credit is not a temporary dislocation — it is a structural, multi-decade shift driven by four converging forces:

  • Regulatory permanence: Basel IV capital rules are phased in through 2028 — bank withdrawal from infrastructure lending is a 10-year dynamic, not a temporary cycle
  • Technology-driven demand: AI compute demand is doubling every 18 months; data center capacity constraints cannot be solved on a short timeline
  • Policy-driven reshoring: Bipartisan consensus on domestic manufacturing has created decade-long incentive structures that are politically durable regardless of election outcomes
  • Energy transition math: The U.S. power grid must double its capacity by 2050 — at current build rates, the financing need is $300B+ per year, far exceeding public market capacity

Backbone is positioned as a first-mover institutional platform in a yield niche that will only grow in size and sophistication over the next decade. Early institutional anchors capture the best of the vintage: widest spreads, strongest collateral selection, and maximum relationship advantage in deal sourcing.

Fund Strategy
Three deeply complementary asset pillars — unified underwriting discipline — single collateral framework

Backbone Infrastructure Credit Fund I operates a disciplined three-pillar senior secured lending strategy. Each pillar is chosen for its complementary characteristics: different rate environments, different collateral types, and different deal sourcing channels — creating natural diversification within a unified underwriting and collateral management framework.

Pillar I · 40% Target Allocation
Data Center & AI Infrastructure Senior Secured Lending

We originate first-lien senior secured construction and term loans to data center developers and colocation operators building or expanding facilities under long-term hyperscaler contracts. Target borrowers are established operators with executed lease agreements with Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, AWS, or Meta — providing contracted revenue as primary repayment collateral, layered over the real property and mechanical/electrical infrastructure of the facility itself.

Deal Characteristics:
  • Loan size: $25M – $150M
  • LTV: 60–65% against fully-stabilized appraisal value
  • Coupon: 10.5% – 13.0% floating (SOFR + 450–700 bps)
  • OID / Origination: 2.0% – 4.0% at closing
  • Term: 3–5 years (aligns with hyperscaler lease terms)
  • Guarantee: Personal or parent company guarantee required on deals under $75M
Deal Sourcing:

CIO relationships with data center developers cultivated through prior infrastructure investment banking advisory work. Proprietary pipeline through three national data center brokers under exclusive sourcing agreements. Referrals from hyperscaler real estate teams seeking financing solutions for their preferred vendors.

Why These Three Pillars — And No Others

Each pillar was selected on three criteria: (1) hard asset collateral with independently verifiable value, (2) contracted or essential-service cash flows that provide a secondary repayment source independent of asset value, and (3) structural barriers to bank financing that create a persistent non-bank premium. No pillar was selected based on return alone.

The three pillars are operationally complementary: they share the same underwriting methodology (hard asset LTV + contracted cash flow coverage), the same collateral enforcement framework (UCC-1 + first-lien mortgage + assignment of contracts), and the same deal team. Portfolio management overhead is minimal because every deal type follows the same credit approval and monitoring framework.

The pillars are also economically diversifying: data center lending is driven by technology demand (AI), energy lending by policy and power demand, and industrial lending by manufacturing supply chain restructuring. The three demand drivers are uncorrelated — a slowdown in one does not impair the others.

Deal Mechanics
How capital is sourced, underwritten, approved, deployed, and monitored across the three pillars

Origination Channels

Proprietary CIO Network

Caleb Caraway's direct relationships with developers, operators, and principals across all three verticals. These are first-call relationships generating deals before they reach the broader market — typically representing 40–55% of annual deal flow by volume.

Platform Proprietary Deal Flow

The Principal's integrated platform — spanning private credit, mineral resources, and multi-strategy fund management — generates proprietary infrastructure deal flow through established institutional relationships, providing first-look access to transactions before they reach the broader market.

Broker & Advisor Channel

Three national data center brokers under sourcing agreements, plus active relationships with CBRE, JLL, and CW industrial capital markets teams. Fee-compensated for funded loans. Generates 30–40% of deal flow.

Developer & Borrower Repeat Business

Established borrowers returning for follow-on financing as their portfolios scale. Targeted to represent 20%+ of annual origination volume by year 3 — the highest quality and lowest cost deal channel available.

Credit Approval Process

Initial Screening (Days 1–3)

CIO screens for: (a) pillar fit, (b) LTV ≤65%, (c) contracted revenue or essential-service demand, (d) borrower track record. Deals failing any screen are declined without further work. Target: ≤30% of submissions advance.

Full Underwriting (Days 4–21)

Independent MAI appraisal ordered. Borrower financial review (3 years audited). Contract/PPA/lease review by SCALE LLP securities counsel. Environmental Phase I ordered. Cash flow model built. DSCR and LTV stress-tested to 3 scenarios.

Investment Committee Approval (Day 22–25)

CIO presents full credit memo to Investment Committee. Approval requires: base case DSCR ≥1.35x, stress case DSCR ≥1.10x, LTV ≤65%, all legal diligence complete. Dissent is documented. Unanimous approval required for loans >$75M.

Legal Documentation & Funding (Days 26–45)

SCALE LLP prepares loan agreement, UCC-1 filings, mortgage/deed of trust, assignment of contracts and rents, environmental indemnity. Title insurance required. Axos Bank funding wire upon confirmation of all document deliverables. NAV Fund Services records position.

Ongoing Monitoring

Monthly borrower financial reporting required. Quarterly site inspection or third-party inspection report. Annual full credit review by CIO. Covenant trigger notification to IC within 24 hours. Workout protocol activated immediately on first technical default.

Collateral Analysis
What backs every dollar — LTV ratios, collateral layers, and enforcement mechanisms by asset pillar
Core Collateral Philosophy: Every loan in the Backbone portfolio is structured so that the collateral alone — without any dependence on refinancing, asset appreciation, or borrower operating performance — can repay principal in full upon enforcement. We underwrite to liquidation value, not going-concern value. We price for income. We structure for recovery. This is not aspirational — it is a hard underwriting rule enforced at every Investment Committee.

Collateral Stack — Per Dollar Lent

Layer 1 — Primary
First Lien Mortgage / Deed of Trust
Recorded first-priority lien on all real property associated with the collateral asset. Title insurance policy (ALTA lender's form) insuring lien priority. Covers 100% of real property value.
Layer 2 — Contracted Revenue
Assignment of Contracts, Rents & Revenues
Collateral assignment of all contracts (hyperscaler leases, PPAs, government contracts), rents, and revenue streams. In default, Backbone steps directly into the revenue flow without requiring asset sale.
Layer 3 — Personal / Corporate
UCC-1 Blanket Lien + Guaranty
UCC-1 financing statement perfecting first-priority lien on all personal property, equipment, IP, and accounts. Personal or corporate guaranty on eligible loans. Cross-collateralization with repeat borrowers.
Layer 4 — Insurance
Required Insurance Coverage
All borrowers required to maintain: property insurance (replacement cost), liability, business interruption, and where applicable, builders risk. Backbone named as loss payee and additional insured. Coverage level covenant throughout loan life.

Stress Test: What Happens in a Severe Recession?

We modeled a severe economic stress scenario (2009-level recession) applied to the target portfolio:

  • Data center occupancy: Hyperscaler demand is counter-cyclical; data consumption grows in recessions. We stress-tested a 15% revenue reduction (conservative). Result: DSCR falls from 1.40x to 1.19x — above 1.10x stress covenant threshold. No defaults triggered at portfolio level.
  • Energy assets: PPAs are "take-or-pay" contracts with investment-grade utilities — recession does not reduce contracted payments. We stress-tested counterparty default risk at 5% of PPA volume. Result: DSCR falls from 1.38x to 1.27x — covenant compliant.
  • Industrial real property: Most vulnerable to recession. We stress-tested 25% occupancy decline and 15% cap rate expansion (reducing value). Result: LTV rises from 57% to 71% on stress-affected assets — triggering cash trap and remediation covenant. Estimated 2–3 loans require workout; estimated losses of 30¢ on the dollar on those positions. Portfolio-level impact: <150 bps reduction in net IRR.

Stress Case Net IRR: 12–14% — still outperforms liquid alternatives in a severe recession, with full capital preservation at portfolio level.

HIGH-IMPACT RISK FACTORS

Credit / Default Risk

Borrowers may fail to make scheduled payments or repay principal at maturity. While the Fund structures every loan with hard asset collateral at ≤65% LTV, distressed asset sales may not recover full principal in all scenarios. In a severe economic downturn affecting multiple asset categories simultaneously, losses could exceed projected equity cushions. The Fund does not carry credit insurance. Investors could lose a portion or all of their invested capital.

Liquidity Risk — Illiquid Investment with No Guaranteed Secondary Market

Interests in the Fund are illiquid. There is no established secondary market, no right of redemption, and no guarantee that a secondary market will develop. Investors should be prepared to hold their commitment for the full 7-year (+ 2-year extension) term. Capital returned is subject to loan repayment by borrowers, which is not guaranteed on schedule.

Concentration Risk — Three Asset Pillars

The Fund's strategy is concentrated in three infrastructure verticals. A structural adverse event affecting one or more of these verticals simultaneously (e.g., hyperscaler contract cancellations + energy market collapse + industrial demand shock) could impair multiple portfolio positions concurrently. Diversification across pillars reduces but does not eliminate correlation risk in a systemic scenario.

Collateral Valuation Risk

Real property valuations may decline materially from levels at origination. Independent MAI appraisals represent point-in-time opinions of value based on market conditions at the time of appraisal. Rapid changes in capitalization rates, technology obsolescence (particularly for data center assets), energy market restructuring, or industrial demand shifts could cause collateral values to fall faster than loan balances amortize.

Interest Rate Risk — SOFR-Linked Loans

Approximately 60–70% of portfolio loans are SOFR-linked floating rate. Significant declines in the SOFR benchmark rate would reduce portfolio income proportionally, reducing distributable cash yield to investors below projections. Rising rates, conversely, may increase borrower default probability on fixed-cost projects. The Fund does not currently intend to employ interest rate hedging instruments.

Deal Flow and Deployment Risk

The Fund targets 3-year full deployment. Failure to originate qualifying loans at pace — due to competitive dynamics, market conditions, or regulatory changes — would result in cash drag on uninvested capital, reducing actual net IRR below projections. Deployment pace is within management's control only to the extent that market conditions support deal flow at underwriting standards.

Key Man Risk

The Fund's investment strategy is substantially dependent on the continued involvement of Caleb Caraway as Principal and CIO. His relationships, judgment, and origination network are central to the Fund's competitive advantage. His departure, disability, or death could materially impair the Fund's ability to source, underwrite, and manage the portfolio. A formal key-man clause in the LPA provides investors with notification rights and the right to accelerate capital return upon a key-man event.

Regulatory Risk — Borrower Compliance

Portfolio assets in regulated industries (energy, industrial manufacturing) are subject to regulatory changes that may impair borrower revenue, increase operating costs, or require capital expenditures not contemplated in original underwriting. Changes to environmental regulations, grid interconnection rules, or industrial permitting requirements could reduce borrower cash flows and impair debt service coverage.

Technology Obsolescence Risk — Data Center Pillar

Data center assets may become partially or fully obsolete due to rapid changes in computing technology, power density requirements, or cooling infrastructure. Assets underwritten to current hyperscaler specifications may not accommodate next-generation compute requirements, potentially impairing lease renewal probability and collateral value at maturity. We partially mitigate this risk by targeting assets with remaining lease terms exceeding loan maturity.

Environmental / Climate Risk

Physical climate risks (flooding, wildfire, severe weather) may damage or destroy collateral assets, impair operations, and increase insurance costs. While Phase I environmental assessments and property insurance requirements are mandatory, comprehensive climate risk assessment is not currently performed at origination. Severe climate events could impair collateral value faster than insurance proceeds can be obtained.

Enforcement Risk — Collateral Recovery Timelines

Enforcement of collateral rights through foreclosure or UCC sale is subject to state law procedures, borrower defenses, and court timelines. In some jurisdictions, non-judicial foreclosure timelines can extend to 6–18 months. During workout periods, collateral may deteriorate in value, and contractual cash flows may be disrupted. While the Fund retains SCALE LLP for enforcement, legal outcomes are inherently uncertain.

IRC §892 — Foreign Government Income Tax Exemption

Section 892 of the Internal Revenue Code provides a complete exemption from U.S. federal income tax for certain types of income received by foreign governments and their controlled entities. This is the cornerstone tax advantage for sovereign wealth fund investors in Backbone Infrastructure Credit Fund I.

What qualifies under §892:

  • Interest income from obligations of U.S. persons — covers all Fund loan interest income
  • Income from the sale or exchange of securities — covers loan exit and prepayment premium income
  • OID recognized over loan life — treated as exempt interest income under §892

Structure designed for §892 optimization:

  • SWF investors subscribe through the Cayman feeder, which holds a limited partnership interest in the Delaware LP
  • The Cayman feeder is structured as a non-participating entity that does not engage in commercial activities — preserving §892 eligibility
  • Portfolio loans are structured as debt instruments so income flows as interest (§892-exempt) rather than ECI
  • SCALE LLP / Dori Karjian provides a §892 eligibility opinion letter as part of subscription documents for each qualifying SWF investor
Result: A qualifying sovereign wealth fund investor in Backbone Infrastructure Credit Fund I pays zero U.S. federal income tax on all interest, OID, and exit income generated by the Fund.

ERISA Plan Asset Analysis

The Fund maintains non-plan asset status under ERISA and DOL Plan Asset Regulations:

  • Fund monitors benefit plan investor (BPI) participation at each capital call and closing
  • BPI ownership maintained below 25% of each class of equity interests
  • SWF investors are NOT classified as benefit plan investors under ERISA §3(42) — they do not count toward the 25% BPI threshold
  • If BPI ownership approaches 20%, the Fund will restrict additional BPI subscriptions to maintain compliance
  • Annual ERISA status certification provided to all investors in the annual report
