Fund Purpose & Structure

Step 3 — LBMA Re-Refining (If Required)

The LBMA Good Delivery standard is the globally recognized institutional benchmark for large gold bars and silver bars traded in the professional market. Good Delivery bars must meet specific weight, purity, and marking specifications set by the LBMA. Gold bars not originating from an LBMA-accredited refinery — including artisanal and small-scale mining output from Ghana, Uganda, and other supply chains — do not inherently meet Good Delivery standard and are not acceptable to institutional buyers at full spot pricing.

To address this, the fund has established access to an LBMA-approved re-refinery in Dubai. All non-Good-Delivery metal sourced through the fund's global supply network is sent to this refinery prior to custody acceptance. The refinery process: (1) verifies metal fineness and content via independent assay; (2) re-melts and re-casts metal to LBMA Good Delivery specification; (3) issues LBMA-compliant vault warrant documentation; and (4) provides provenance attestation that constitutes the fund's responsible sourcing record for that metal.

The cost of re-refining is factored into purchase pricing — the effective net purchase price to the fund accounts for refinery cost, ensuring target gross margins are achieved on a fully-burdened basis.

Commodity-backed lending at 15–20% on fully secured positions would appear anomalous in traditional credit markets, where equivalent-quality collateral might command 6–8% financing rates. The explanation lies in market access, not credit quality.

Traditional bank lenders and large private credit funds face three barriers to commodity-backed lending: (1) they lack vault relationships and operational infrastructure to accept, monitor, and liquidate physical commodity collateral; (2) their compliance and regulatory frameworks are not optimized for commodity-specific collateral types; and (3) their standard credit analysis models are not calibrated for businesses where the primary asset is a commodity priced by an international spot market. These barriers eliminate the vast majority of institutional competition, enabling Arabian Capital Partners to charge 15–20% on loans that are, from a collateral quality and liquidity standpoint, among the most fully secured credits available in any asset class.

Why Zero Fee Is Structurally Sustainable

Zero fee structures are rare at institutional scale because most fund managers operate in markets where the spread between gross portfolio yield and investor yield is too thin to fund operations without explicit fee revenue. If a private credit fund generates 10–12% gross and pays investors 7–8%, the 2–4% spread barely covers compliance, operations, and personnel — hence the 2% management fee.

Arabian Capital Partners targets 35–55% gross yield on deployed capital. After paying investors 14–20%, the fund retains approximately 15–35% operating margin. On a $1B fund, this represents approximately $97M–$280M in annual retained operating margin before any fee. The fund's operations are fully self-funded from this margin — management, operations, compliance, service providers, and profit — without requiring a single dollar of fee extraction from investor capital or returns. The zero-fee structure is not a marketing decision. It is an arithmetic consequence of the fund's gross yield profile relative to its investor yield obligations.

It is based on the OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas (OECD Guidance) and is aligned with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights.

The RGG requires gold refiners — and by extension, entities supplying gold to LBMA refiners — to implement a five-step OECD due diligence framework:

1

Establish Strong Company Management Systems

Policies on ethical sourcing; senior management oversight; record-keeping and documentation procedures; supplier code of conduct requirements.

2

Identify and Assess Risks in the Supply Chain

Mapping of supply chain from mine to refinery; identification of conflict-affected and high-risk areas in sourcing corridors; red flag screening for prohibited behaviors.

3

Design and Implement a Strategy to Respond to Identified Risks

Immediate suspension of engagement with suppliers where serious risks are identified; remediation requirements for medium-risk situations; ongoing monitoring of supplier conduct.

4

Carry Out Independent Third-Party Audit of Supply Chain Due Diligence

LBMA-accredited refiners are subject to independent audit of their responsible sourcing practices; re-refined metal is certified by an audited LBMA refinery.

5

Report on Supply Chain Due Diligence

Annual public disclosure by LBMA refiners of their responsible sourcing practices and audit outcomes; documentation maintained for investor and regulatory review.

By routing all non-Good-Delivery metal through the fund's Dubai LBMA-approved re-refinery, Arabian Capital Partners integrates its operations into this certified framework. The re-refinery's LBMA accreditation means it has undergone and passed independent audit of its responsible sourcing practices. Metal that the refinery accepts and re-certifies has passed through an audited supply chain ethics process.

Regulation D — Rule 506(c)

The fund is offered pursuant to Rule 506(c) of Regulation D under the Securities Act of 1933, which provides an exemption from SEC registration for private placements. Rule 506(c) permits general solicitation and general advertising of the offering — the fund may be marketed publicly — provided that all actual purchasers are verified Accredited Investors. At Arabian Capital Partners, all investors must be Qualified Purchasers (a higher standard than Accredited Investor), automatically satisfying 506(c) verification requirements. Rule 506(c) offerings must file Form D with the SEC within 15 days of the first sale of securities. No state securities registration is required under Rule 506(c) preemption, though state notice filings may be required in certain states.

Section 3(c)(7) — Investment Company Act

The fund relies on the Section 3(c)(7) exemption from registration as an investment company under the Investment Company Act of 1940. This exemption is available to funds that: (1) do not make a public offering of securities; and (2) limit beneficial owners exclusively to Qualified Purchasers. Section 3(c)(7) imposes no limit on the number of investors — unlike Section 3(c)(1), which is capped at 100 investors. The fund's use of 3(c)(7) instead of 3(c)(1) is a deliberate structural choice that allows it to accept any number of Qualified Purchaser investors — enabling all seven target sovereign wealth funds to invest simultaneously without structural constraint — and allows the fund to scale AUM from $50M to $5B+ without any amendment, re-filing, or structural modification.

A safe harbor under the Securities Act of 1933 allowing private funds to conduct general solicitation (public advertising) of their offering, provided that all actual purchasers are verified Accredited Investors or better. Arabian Capital Partners requires Qualified Purchaser status (higher than Accredited Investor), satisfying 506(c) requirements.
Accredited Investor
Under SEC rules, an individual with $1M+ net worth or $200K+ annual income ($300K joint), or an institution with $5M+ in assets, among other categories. A lower qualification standard than Qualified Purchaser. Arabian Capital Partners requires Qualified Purchaser status.
Chain of Custody
In commodity trading, the documented record of all entities who have handled or possessed physical metal from the point of mining or refinement through to the ultimate buyer. A complete and unbroken chain of custody is required for LBMA Good Delivery compliance and responsible sourcing verification.
CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange)
The world's largest financial derivatives exchange, which sets standards for approved precious metals depositories and warehouses used in futures contract delivery. CME-approved vaults are institutional-grade storage facilities acceptable for use as loan collateral in the fund's commodity credit tranche.
DMCC (Dubai Multi Commodities Centre)
A free trade zone in Dubai and one of the world's largest commodity trading hubs. Commodity traders operating in the DMCC are licensed, regulated, and subject to DMCC's commodity trading rules and AML requirements. The fund's Dubai buyer relationship operates within the DMCC ecosystem.
EITI (Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative)
An international standard for transparency in oil, gas, and mining sectors. EITI member countries — including Ghana and Uganda — commit to publishing information about taxes, licenses, and revenues from extractive industries, supporting supply chain traceability.
FATF (Financial Action Task Force)
An intergovernmental body that sets international standards for anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing. FATF member status and compliance rating indicate a country's adherence to AML/CFT standards. The fund applies FATF screening to all sourcing counterparties and transactions.
Form D
A brief notice filed with the SEC within 15 days of the first sale of securities in a Regulation D private offering. Not a registration statement — it does not subject the fund to SEC review or approval. It is a public notice of the offering's existence and basic terms.
Good Delivery (LBMA Good Delivery)
The LBMA's standard specifying the physical characteristics of gold and silver bars acceptable for settlement in the professional OTC market. Good Delivery gold bars: 350–430 troy ounces, minimum 99.5% fineness, produced by an LBMA-accredited refiner. Good Delivery bars can be sold to any institutional buyer globally at full spot pricing without additional verification.
K-1 (Schedule K-1)
An IRS tax form issued annually by a partnership (including an LLC taxed as a partnership) to each partner/member, reporting their allocable share of the entity's income, deductions, credits, and other tax items. Investors in Arabian Capital Partners receive K-1s from Spicer Jeffries LLP annually for U.S. tax reporting purposes.
LBMA (London Bullion Market Association)
The international trade association for the global OTC gold and silver market. Sets the Good Delivery standard, accredits refineries, and publishes the Responsible Gold Guidance. LBMA certification is the globally recognized institutional standard for physical precious metals — required for acceptance by central banks, sovereign wealth funds, and institutional buyers.
LTV (Loan-to-Value Ratio)
In the commodity credit tranche, the ratio of the outstanding loan balance to the current spot market value of the pledged physical collateral. Maximum LTV at origination: 75%. Margin call triggered at 80% LTV. At 80% LTV, a 20% further decline in collateral value would be required before the fund experiences any principal impairment — providing substantial protection against commodity price movements.
NAV (Net Asset Value)
The fund's total assets (physical metal in custody at spot value + outstanding loan balances + cash) minus total liabilities (accrued but unpaid investor yield obligations). Calculated monthly by NAV Fund Services.
OECD Due Diligence Guidance
The OECD's five-step framework for responsible supply chain management of minerals from conflict-affected and high-risk areas, upon which the LBMA Responsible Gold Guidance is based. Widely adopted by institutional investors, including NBIM, as the applicable standard for evaluating commodity supply chain ethics.
PFIC (Passive Foreign Investment Company)
A non-U.S. entity that is treated as a PFIC for U.S. tax purposes if 75%+ of its income is passive or 50%+ of its assets produce passive income. Special U.S. tax rules apply to U.S. investors in PFICs. Spicer Jeffries LLP will analyze and disclose PFIC status annually. Arabian Capital Partners, as an active trade finance fund, is not expected to qualify as a PFIC, but analysis will be provided.
Qualified Purchaser (QP)
Defined under Section 2(a)(51) of the Investment Company Act of 1940. The standard applicable to 3(c)(7) fund investors. Higher threshold than Accredited Investor. For institutions: must own and invest at least $100,000,000 in investments. For individuals: must own not less than $25,000,000 in investments. All seven target sovereign wealth funds qualify as institutional QPs under the $100M+ standard.
Trade Finance
A financial mechanism that facilitates the purchase and sale of physical goods across the global supply chain by providing short-term financing for the holding period between purchase and sale. In Arabian Capital Partners' context: the fund provides its own capital to purchase physical precious metals, holds them in custody for 3–5 days, and sells to institutional buyers — earning the gross margin on each transaction. The fund does not lend to third parties in the trade finance tranche; it is the principal buyer and seller.
UCC-1 Financing Statement
A legal document filed under the Uniform Commercial Code to publicly perfect a security interest in personal property (including physical commodities) offered as loan collateral. Filing a UCC-1 gives the lender (Arabian Capital Partners) a publicly recorded priority claim on the collateral ahead of unsecured creditors and subsequent lienholders. Combined with a vault warrant pledge, the UCC-1 provides the fund with enforceable, first-priority collateral rights on all credit tranche loans.
Vault Warrant
A document issued by an LBMA-certified vault operator (Brinks, Transguard) evidencing title to specific physical metal bars held in the vault. A vault warrant is a negotiable document — it can be pledged as collateral, transferred by endorsement, and used to direct delivery of the metal. In the fund's credit tranche, borrowers pledge vault warrants to the fund as collateral for loans; the fund holds endorsed warrants with authority to direct liquidation upon default.
