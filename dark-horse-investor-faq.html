Section 1 — Platform Overview
The Dark Horse Platform
01What is Dark Horse Wealth Management?

Dark Horse Wealth Management LLC is a private investment platform headquartered in the United States. It serves as the parent organization overseeing a family of private debt funds, each structured as a separate limited liability company with its own investment mandate, manager, and regulatory filing. The platform's mission is to originate, structure, and manage debt investments across multiple asset classes while providing investors with contractual fixed-rate returns.

02Who manages the platform and its funds?

The platform is led by Caleb Caraway, who serves as the Managing Member of Dark Horse Wealth Management LLC and is the principal across all three funds. Each fund has its own dedicated management entity: Corinthian Capital Partners LLC manages Corinthian Capital Fund LLC, Draft Horse Wealth Management LLC manages Draft Horse Capital Partners LLC, and Quarter Horse Wealth Management LLC manages Quarter Horse Capital Partners LLC. This structure ensures operational separation while maintaining unified strategic oversight.

03How many funds operate under the Dark Horse umbrella?

Three funds currently operate under the platform: Corinthian Capital Fund LLC (Fund I — operational, private lending), Draft Horse Capital Partners LLC (Fund II — multi-strategy debt, $1B target), and Quarter Horse Capital Partners LLC (Fund III — deal-by-deal debt, $120M target). Each fund is a standalone legal entity with its own PPM, subscription documents, and Form D filing.

04What is the overarching investment philosophy?

All three funds are debt funds. Investors provide capital that is deployed as structured debt across various asset classes. In return, investors receive fixed contractual rates of return as defined in each fund's PPM. The platform does not operate equity co-investment vehicles. The philosophy centers on capital preservation through disciplined underwriting, contractual cash flows, and diversification across uncorrelated sectors.

05What are the key service providers?

The platform engages institutional-quality service providers across all three funds: SCALE LLP serves as legal counsel and prepares all PPMs and subscription documents, Spicer & Jefferies serves as the independent auditor and tax advisor, and NAV Fund Services provides third-party fund administration including NAV calculations, investor reporting, and capital account maintenance.

06Is Dark Horse Wealth Management a registered investment adviser?

DH Advisers LLC, the advisory arm of the platform, is currently in the process of becoming a registered investment adviser (RIA) with the appropriate regulatory authority. Until registration is finalized, each fund operates under applicable private fund exemptions. Investors should refer to each fund's PPM for the current regulatory status and any applicable exemptions relied upon.

07How are the three funds differentiated from one another?

Each fund targets a different segment of the private debt market. Corinthian Capital Fund is an operational fund focused on private lending to cash-flowing e-commerce businesses at a fixed 2% monthly / 24% annual rate. Draft Horse Capital Partners is a large-scale multi-strategy debt fund targeting $1B across five verticals. Quarter Horse Capital Partners is a more concentrated deal-by-deal debt fund targeting $120M, managed exclusively by Caleb Caraway with full discretion over every deployment. All three share a common commitment to zero management fees and zero performance fees.

08What is the minimum investment across the funds?

Minimum investments vary by fund. Corinthian Capital Fund and Draft Horse Capital Partners each set their own minimums as detailed in their respective PPMs. Quarter Horse Capital Partners has a minimum investment of $50,000. The General Partner of each fund retains discretion to waive or reduce minimums on a case-by-case basis. Prospective investors should consult the relevant subscription documents for the most current terms.

Section 2 — Corinthian Capital Fund LLC
Fund I — Private Lending (Operational)
09What is Corinthian Capital Fund LLC?

Corinthian Capital Fund LLC is a private debt fund and the first operational fund within the Dark Horse platform. Managed by Corinthian Capital Partners LLC, it focuses on originating private credit facilities to cash-flowing e-commerce businesses. The fund offers investors a fixed contractual return of 2% per month (24% per annum). It is currently operational and accepting subscriptions from qualified investors.

10What is the fund's investment strategy?

The fund deploys capital as structured private loans to established, cash-flowing e-commerce businesses. These are operating companies with demonstrable revenue histories and identifiable cash flow. The fund's underwriting process evaluates borrower financials, revenue trends, operating margins, and repayment capacity before committing capital. All loan terms, covenants, and repayment schedules are negotiated on a deal-by-deal basis.

11What return does Corinthian offer investors?

Investors in Corinthian Capital Fund receive a fixed contractual rate of 2% per month, equivalent to 24% per annum. This rate is established in the fund's PPM and subscription documents. Returns are contractual obligations of the fund to its investors, paid from the fund's lending revenues and cash reserves. The fixed-rate structure means investor returns do not fluctuate with market conditions.

12What regulatory exemption does Corinthian operate under?

Corinthian Capital Fund LLC conducts its offering under Regulation D, Rule 506(b) of the Securities Act of 1933. Under 506(b), the fund may accept investments from an unlimited number of accredited investors and up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited investors, but may not engage in general solicitation or general advertising. The fund has filed its Form D with the SEC.

13Who are the borrowers in the Corinthian fund?

Borrowers are cash-flowing e-commerce businesses — operating companies that generate recurring revenue through online commerce channels. The fund does not lend to startups, pre-revenue businesses, or speculative ventures. Each borrower undergoes the fund's proprietary underwriting process, which evaluates historical financial performance, revenue consistency, margin stability, and overall repayment capacity before any capital is committed.

14How is investor capital protected in Corinthian?

Capital protection in the Corinthian fund is achieved through rigorous underwriting discipline, conservative loan-to-value structuring, and active portfolio management. The fund maintains reserves and enforces loan covenants that provide early warning of borrower distress. Specific collateral arrangements, if any, are negotiated on a deal-by-deal basis and documented in each loan agreement. Investors should review the PPM for complete details on risk factors and capital protection mechanisms.

Important: No investment is risk-free. Private lending carries credit risk, liquidity risk, and the risk of borrower default. Past performance of any borrower is not indicative of future results. Please review the fund's PPM for a complete discussion of risk factors.

Corinthian Capital Fund is structured as a private fund with limited liquidity. Investments are generally illiquid, and investors should be prepared to hold their positions for the duration specified in the PPM. Any withdrawal or redemption provisions, including notice periods and potential limitations, are detailed in the fund's governing documents. Section 5 — Fees & Economics
Fee Structure & GP Alignment
33What fees does Dark Horse charge investors?

Zero management fees. Zero performance fees. Zero carried interest. This applies across all three funds — Corinthian Capital Fund, Draft Horse Capital Partners, and Quarter Horse Capital Partners. There are no asset-based management fees, no incentive allocations, and no carried interest charged to investors. The funds' operating expenses (legal, audit, administration, and similar third-party costs) are borne by the funds as detailed in each PPM, but no fees are charged to investors for the management of their capital.

34Why does the platform charge zero fees?

The zero-fee structure reflects a deliberate alignment philosophy. Traditional fund fee structures — typically "2 and 20" — create a misalignment where managers earn fees regardless of performance. By eliminating all management and performance fees, the Dark Horse platform ensures that the General Partner's economic interests are fully aligned with investor outcomes. The GP generates returns solely through the spread between what the fund earns on its deployed capital and what it pays investors, creating a direct incentive to underwrite conservatively and manage actively.

35How does the GP generate revenue if there are no fees?

The General Partner's economics come from the net spread between the fund's lending revenue and the contractual rates paid to investors. For example, if a fund earns a higher rate on its deployed capital than the fixed rate it owes investors, the excess accrues to the GP. This structure means the GP only profits when loans perform and generate returns above the investor's contractual rate — a fundamentally different incentive than fee-based compensation, which rewards asset gathering regardless of investment outcomes.

36Are there any fund-level expenses passed through to investors?

While the funds charge no management or performance fees, each fund does bear its own reasonable operating expenses as customary for private funds. These typically include legal expenses (SCALE LLP), audit and tax preparation (Spicer & Jefferies), fund administration (NAV Fund Services), regulatory filing costs, and other third-party professional fees. All expense categories and limitations are disclosed in each fund's PPM. These are standard fund-level expenses, not additional fees charged by the GP.

37Is the zero-fee structure permanent or subject to change?

The fee terms for each fund are established in its governing documents and PPM. Any material changes to the fee structure would require the procedures specified in each fund's operating agreement, which may include investor notice, consent, or other protective provisions. Investors should review the specific amendment provisions in their fund's governing documents. The current zero-fee structure is a foundational element of the platform's investment philosophy and value proposition.

38How does the zero-fee approach compare to industry norms?

The traditional private fund fee model charges a 1.5–2.0% annual management fee on committed or invested capital plus 15–20% carried interest above a hurdle rate. On a $100M fund, this means $1.5–2.0M annually in management fees alone — paid regardless of performance — plus a significant share of any profits. The Dark Horse zero-fee model eliminates both layers entirely. Investors retain 100% of their contractual returns, and the GP earns only from the spread on performing investments. This creates one of the most investor-friendly fee structures in the private debt market.

Section 6 — Regulatory & Compliance
Securities Exemptions, Filings & Regulatory Status
39What is the difference between 506(b) and 506(c)?

Both are exemptions under Regulation D of the Securities Act of 1933 that allow private funds to raise capital without SEC registration. Rule 506(b) permits offerings to accredited investors and up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited investors, but prohibits general solicitation and general advertising. Rule 506(c) permits general solicitation and advertising, but requires that all investors be verified accredited investors through reasonable verification steps. Corinthian and Quarter Horse operate under 506(b); Draft Horse operates under 506(c).

40What is the 3(c)(1) exemption and how does it apply?

Section 3(c)(1) of the Investment Company Act of 1940 provides an exemption from registration as an investment company for funds that have no more than 100 beneficial owners and do not make (or propose to make) a public offering of their securities. The Dark Horse funds rely on Section 3(c)(1) (or similar applicable exemptions) to avoid investment company registration, which allows them to operate as private funds. This limits each fund to a maximum of 100 beneficial owners, as defined under the Act.

41Have the funds filed Form D with the SEC?

Yes. Each fund — Corinthian Capital Fund LLC, Draft Horse Capital Partners LLC, and Quarter Horse Capital Partners LLC — has filed its Form D with the Securities and Exchange Commission as required under Regulation D. Form D is a notice filing that provides the SEC with basic information about the fund, its management, and the offering. State-level notice filings (blue sky filings) are completed as required by applicable state securities laws. Copies of Form D filings are publicly available through the SEC's EDGAR system.

Section 7 — Risk Factors
Key Risks for Multi-Strategy Debt Funds
45What are the principal risks of investing in the Dark Horse funds?

All investments in private debt funds carry inherent risks. Principal risks across the Dark Horse platform include: credit risk (borrowers may default on their obligations), liquidity risk (investments are illiquid and cannot be readily sold or redeemed), concentration risk (exposure to specific borrowers, sectors, or deal types), market risk (macroeconomic conditions may adversely affect borrower performance), and manager risk (the funds depend on the skill and judgment of their managers). Investors may lose some or all of their invested capital. Each fund's PPM contains a comprehensive discussion of risk factors specific to that fund.

Important: Investment in any Dark Horse fund involves a high degree of risk and is suitable only for investors who can bear the loss of their entire investment. Past performance is not indicative of future results. Prospective investors must read the complete risk factors section of the relevant PPM before investing.
46How does diversification across sectors mitigate risk?

Draft Horse Capital Partners' multi-vertical structure is designed to reduce the impact of any single sector downturn on the overall portfolio. Aviation, mining, real estate, e-commerce, and fine art each respond differently to economic cycles, interest rate environments, and market conditions. A downturn in one sector may be offset by stability or growth in another. However, diversification does not eliminate risk — broad economic downturns, systemic credit events, or correlated shocks could adversely affect multiple verticals simultaneously. Diversification is a risk management tool, not a guarantee against loss.

47What are the risks specific to private lending and credit?

Private lending carries risks that differ from public market fixed-income investments. Borrowers in the private market may have limited financial history, less diversified revenue streams, and fewer refinancing options than public issuers. Key credit risks include borrower default, covenant violations, delayed repayment, business deterioration, and inadequate collateral coverage. The funds' underwriting processes are designed to identify and mitigate these risks, but no underwriting process can eliminate credit risk entirely. Recovery rates on defaulted private loans may vary significantly depending on deal structure and borrower circumstances.

48What is the liquidity risk for investors?

All three funds are structured as private funds with limited liquidity. There is no public market for fund interests, and investors should not expect to be able to sell or transfer their interests readily. Withdrawal and redemption rights, if any, are subject to the terms and limitations set forth in each fund's operating agreement and PPM, which may include lock-up periods, notice requirements, and fund-level liquidity constraints. Investors should only commit capital they can afford to have illiquid for the duration of the investment period.

49What is manager or key-person risk?

The success of the Dark Horse funds depends significantly on the skill, judgment, and continued involvement of their managers — particularly Caleb Caraway, who serves as the principal across all three funds and is the sole manager of Quarter Horse Capital Partners. The loss of key personnel, a change in management capacity, or any impairment of the managers' ability to perform their duties could adversely affect fund performance. Investors should consider key-person risk as part of their overall assessment. The PPMs may contain key-person provisions that address this risk.

50Are there sector-specific risks for Draft Horse's verticals?

Yes. Each of Draft Horse's five verticals carries sector-specific risks. Aviation: regulatory changes, fleet obsolescence, and airline industry volatility. Mining & Energy: commodity price fluctuations, environmental regulation, and operational hazards. Real Estate: property value declines, vacancy risk, and interest rate sensitivity. E-Commerce: competitive pressures, platform dependency, and consumer spending shifts. Fine Art: valuation subjectivity, illiquidity of art assets, and market sentiment. These sector risks interact with the general risks described above. Investors should review the PPM for a comprehensive treatment of each vertical's risk profile.
