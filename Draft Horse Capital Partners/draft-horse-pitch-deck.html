FAA
Registry
FAA Civil Aviation Registry — 49 U.S.C. § 44107 / 14 C.F.R. Part 49
For aviation collateral specifically, U.S. law requires conveyance notation on the FAA Civil Aviation Registry in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma, to perfect a security interest in U.S.-registered civil aircraft. This filing is separate from and in addition to the UCC-1, and it is the legally controlling instrument for priority of claims in U.S.-registered aircraft. An unfiled interest — regardless of the underlying agreement — is unenforceable against a bona fide purchaser for value without notice. The Fund's FAA Registry filing eliminates this risk entirely. For international enforcement contexts, SCALE LLP evaluates applicability of the Cape Town Convention (Aircraft Protocol), which provides additional cross-border priority protection for interests in qualifying aircraft in contracting states.

§892 Framework — Key Questions for SWF Counsel
What is IRC §892?
A U.S. Internal Revenue Code provision exempting foreign governments (including SWFs that qualify as instrumentalities of a foreign government) from U.S. federal income tax on certain categories of U.S.-sourced income — including interest, dividends, and gains from certain investments.
Who qualifies?
Foreign governments and their wholly-owned instrumentalities and agencies. Most sovereign wealth funds (ADQ, ADIA, GIC, PIF, Mubadala, SAFE, etc.) qualify as government-controlled entities — but specific legal analysis by sovereign's U.S. tax counsel is required for each investor.
What income is exempt?
Interest on debt obligations (e.g., Fund distributions from credit facility interest income), gains from disposition of qualifying assets, and dividends from qualifying investments — subject to the nature of Fund income and specific investor facts. Income from commercial activities or UBTI is generally excluded from §892 treatment.
What is the impact?
For a qualifying SWF investor, federal income tax on U.S.-sourced Fund income may be eliminated entirely — converting a 17% pre-tax target yield into a 17% after-tax yield at the federal level. No U.S. withholding tax on qualifying distributions. Material net return enhancement vs. taxable investors.
What does Draft Horse provide?
The Fund's structure and income characterization has been developed with awareness of §892 considerations. Draft Horse will work with sovereign investors to provide the income characterization data required for §892 analysis. The Fund makes no representation regarding tax treatment — each sovereign investor's U.S. tax counsel must confirm applicability.
