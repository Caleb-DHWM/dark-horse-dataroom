Investment Thesis
Why private credit, why sponsor-backed private lending, and why the structural setup in Draft Horse is the highest-conviction expression of this thesis

The Thesis in One Sentence

Banks have permanently retreated from middle-market PE lending under Basel III/IV capital constraints; $3.9 trillion in private equity dry powder is sitting idle waiting for flexible credit solutions; and private credit managers who show up with committed capital, deep borrower relationships, and collateral discipline are capturing yield premiums at multi-decade highs — Draft Horse enters this environment with all three advantages already in place.

The Structural Void Banks Left Behind

The Basel III regulatory cycle — implemented progressively from 2010 through full adoption in the mid-2020s — imposed significantly higher risk-weighted capital requirements on bank lending to middle-market and sponsor-backed borrowers. The economics of this lending category became structurally unattractive for regulated banks: the yield compression from competitive pricing, combined with elevated regulatory capital charges, made it mathematically difficult for banks to earn acceptable returns on equity. This was not a cyclical retreat — it was a permanent structural withdrawal driven by regulatory economics that will not reverse.

The void left by this withdrawal is where private credit has grown from a niche strategy to a $1.7+ trillion asset class in under a decade. The managers who moved into this space earliest — those with existing borrower relationships, deep credit expertise, and institutional-grade operations — captured the most attractive terms and the deepest collateral packages. Draft Horse enters this market not as a latecomer chasing compressed spreads, but as a collateral-disciplined direct lender with an existing $100M deployment relationship already structured and ready to execute.

Why Secured Sponsor-Backed Lending Specifically

  • Borrower quality floor: PE sponsors are institutional operators with structured financials, professional management, and reputational equity at stake. Default carries career consequences for a sponsor, not just financial ones.
  • Identifiable collateral: PE sponsors have specific, owned, appraised assets — not just future cash flows from speculative operations. The Fund lends against what exists, not what is projected.
  • UCC-1 enforcement superiority: Perfected lien gives the Fund legally superior rights to collateral assets in any insolvency scenario — substantially higher recovery than general creditors.
  • Relationship monitoring: Direct sponsor relationship means real-time covenant visibility, no intermediary, and maximum early warning on any credit deterioration.
  • No auction dynamics: Bilateral relationship means no competitive bid process, no broker fees, and no premium paid for deal access.Default is doubly painful for the sponsor.
  • Aviation hard assets as terminal collateral — current G4-class charter fleet provides present, perfected collateral coverage; as Quarter Horse Capital Partners acquires G550 aircraft for the same operator, Draft Horse gains a growing aviation collateral enhancement layer (subordinated to Quarter Horse's first-priority interest)
  • Aviation equity position — Draft Horse holds equity in the charter operating entity. As the G550 fleet is built out by Quarter Horse, this equity position gains value in proportion to fleet growth, above and beyond the credit spread.The Fund does not rely on market conditions to generate returns; it relies on a specific, documented, bilateral borrower relationship, a structured collateral package, and a principal who has deployed his own capital alongside investors in private credit since the Fund's predecessor entity began operations.

Post-Basel III/IV, banks face significantly elevated risk-weighted capital requirements on middle-market and sponsor-backed loans. Banks are not "retreating" temporarily due to risk aversion — they are repricing the activity out of their business model permanently.

The resulting supply-demand imbalance is the foundational reason private credit yields are where they are. When a $10B institutional PE sponsor cannot get a bank to write a competitive middle-market facility, they turn to direct lenders. Why 36 Months? — The Structural Case for the 3-Year Term

The 36-month investor commitment is a deliberate structural decision that creates alignment simultaneously across investors, the Fund, and the borrower.   • Rate locked at entry: Your tier rate is fixed at subscription and does not reset. In a declining rate environment, you continue earning at your locked rate. In a competitive landscape where yields compress, early investors are protected on the downside permanently.
  • Premium for commitment: The 9%–17% yield is explicitly priced to compensate for the liquidity premium. Shorter-duration capital cannot command this yield in any comparable instrument. The term is the source of the premium, not a cost of the premium.

What the 36-Month Term Does for the Fund and Borrower

  • No redemption pressure: The Fund cannot be forced to liquidate positions to meet redemptions. This enables full, immediate deployment without cash drag — every dollar earns from day one.
  • Matched funding architecture: The Fund deploys on 36-month tranches. Matching investor duration creates a perfect asset-liability match — eliminating the maturity mismatch that has caused structural failures in other private credit vehicles.
  • Stronger covenant negotiation: Stable committed capital enables the Fund to negotiate tighter covenants and better terms from borrowers. A fund vulnerable to redemption is a structurally weaker counterparty at the negotiating table.
  • Borrower operational certainty: A 36-month credit facility gives the PE sponsor certainty for long-term operational planning, capital investment, and growth — which is precisely why they're willing to pay the premium rate that generates the investor yield.
Bottom Line: The 36-month term is the structural engine that makes 9%–17% yield achievable and sustainable. Investors are compensated, generously, for the stability their commitment provides. The lock-up is not a cost — it is the reason the yield exists.

"Six Layers of Downside Protection — This Is Not Typical Private Credit"

Most private credit funds have one collateral layer — operating cash flows, or a real estate mortgage, or a stock pledge. Some have two.The Fund does not need all six to succeed. The Fund needs exactly one layer to hold in a default scenario to recover principal in full. The other five are structural redundancy that virtually no single-mandate fund at this scale has bothered to build.
Layer 1 — First Strike
PE Portfolio Company Cash Flows

What it is: A perfected assignment of all cash flow rights and receivables generated by the PE sponsor's operating portfolio companies. Under UCC §9-607, upon a default event, the Fund delivers written notice to all of the borrower's account debtors — the entities that owe money to the borrower — redirecting all outstanding and future payments directly to the Fund.

Why it's powerful: Cash flow enforcement is the fastest-acting tool in the collateral package. It does not require physical seizure of assets, court intervention, or complex sale processes. The Fund intercepts income at the source, within 30–60 days of acceleration. This layer is the "first strike" in any enforcement scenario — it generates immediate cash and preserves the Fund's liquidity while longer-duration asset enforcement proceeds in parallel.

Monitoring: Cash flows are monitored on an ongoing basis through fund administration and direct borrower reporting covenants. Any material deviation from projected cash flow triggers a covenant review prior to any payment default.

Layer 2 — Physical Asset Base
Brick-and-Mortar Real Estate & Inventory

What it is: UCC-1 perfected security interest against all tangible physical assets of the PE sponsor's portfolio companies — commercial and operational real estate, business inventory, equipment, fixtures, and all physical property. This encompasses every physical asset the borrower owns, from the buildings they operate in to the inventory stacked in their warehouses.

Why it's powerful: Physical assets are independent of operating performance. Even if cash flows compress due to a business cycle, the real estate and inventory have standalone appraised values that do not move in direct correlation to EBITDA. In a Texas commercial real estate market that is actively appreciating due to corporate relocation and population growth, this layer's value is increasing over the term of the loan, not decreasing.

Enforcement: Under UCC §9-610, the Fund may arrange commercially reasonable public or private disposition of encumbered inventory and personal property. Real estate disposition proceeds apply against principal and accrued interest.

Layer 3 — Control Override
100% Portfolio Company Equity Pledge

What it is: A pledge of 100% of the PE sponsor's equity interests in all portfolio company holdings. In a default event, the Fund does not merely claim against assets from the outside — the Fund steps into ownership control of the operating businesses themselves. This is the nuclear enforcement option: the Fund becomes the operator, with full authority to manage, restructure, or dispose of the underlying businesses in its entirety.

Why it's powerful: Most private credit funds get a lien against assets. Draft Horse gets ownership rights. If the PE sponsor defaults and the operating businesses are healthy — generating cash, holding valuable assets — the Fund can take control, continue operations, and extract full value over time rather than forcing a distressed fire sale. The equity pledge is a recovery-maximizing tool, not merely an enforcement mechanism.

    Draft Horse Capital Partners offers its interests pursuant to Rule 506(c) of Regulation D under the Securities Act of 1933. Rule 506(c) permits issuers to:

    • Engage in general solicitation and general advertising in connection with an offering

    • Avoid SEC registration and associated ongoing reporting requirements

    In exchange, Rule 506(c) requires the issuer to take reasonable steps to verify that all investors are, in fact, accredited. Self-certification alone is legally insufficient under 506(c).

    IRC §892 Sovereign Wealth Fund Exemption Pathway

    Section 892 of the Internal Revenue Code provides a significant exemption from U.S. federal income tax for sovereign wealth funds and foreign government investors on certain categories of U.S.-source investment income. This exemption is a critical structural consideration for SWF allocators evaluating an investment in Draft Horse Capital Partners, and is addressed here in detail because every SWF investment committee will require confirmation of this analysis before approving an allocation.

    The Core §892 Rule — Plain Language Summary

    Income received by a foreign government (including a sovereign wealth fund that qualifies as an integral part of or controlled entity of a foreign government) from investments in the United States is exempt from U.S. federal income tax under IRC §892, provided that the income is derived from investments in U.S. stocks, bonds, other securities, financial instruments, or bank deposits — and provided that the SWF is not engaged in commercial activities in the United States.

    In the context of a U.S. partnership (such as Draft Horse Capital Partners LLC, structured as a pass-through entity), a qualifying SWF's distributive share of the partnership's investment income is exempt from U.S. withholding tax under §892, provided that (i) the SWF qualifies as a foreign government or integral part / controlled entity thereof; (ii) the partnership income is of a qualifying investment type; and (iii) the SWF is not engaged in commercial activity that would taint the §892 exemption. Treasury Regulations §1.892-3T and §1.892-5T govern the specific technical requirements for this exemption.

    Who Qualifies for §892 Exemption

    • Foreign sovereign governments — ministries, agencies, and departments acting in a governmental capacity
    • Integral parts of a foreign sovereign — entities whose income inures to the government and that are not separately organized outside the government; no private parties may benefit from their income
    • Controlled entities — separately organized entities (including SWFs organized as separate investment authorities) where: (i) the government holds all beneficial interests; (ii) the entity's income is set aside for governmental purposes; and (iii) the entity does not engage in commercial activity
    • Examples of qualifying entities (general — confirm with own counsel): Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA), Government Investment Corporation of Singapore (GIC), Public Investment Fund of Saudi Arabia (PIF), Mubadala Investment Company, Temasek Holdings, Norwegian Government Pension Fund Global (NBIM), Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA), and equivalent sovereign investment vehicles

    STEP 1 · Day 0–5
    Cash Flows & Receivables — Immediate Redirect

    What happens: The Fund activates Account Control Agreements already on file with the Borrower's bank. All incoming payments — charter revenues, portfolio company distributions, rents, and any other receivables — are automatically redirected to the Fund's designated account.     Why it's fast: The Account Control Agreement is signed at closing, not at default. The Fund doesn't need to go to court. It notifies the bank, and the bank executes the redirect. This is the same mechanism used by private lenders at JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs.

    Why it matters: The insurance claim runs in parallel with all other recovery steps. Insurance proceeds are independent of collateral asset values — the insurer pays on the occurrence of the default event, not on the outcome of the asset liquidation. It is an additional, uncorrelated recovery source.

    Aviation Fleet — Hard Asset Liquidation

    What happens: The Fund appoints a FAA-registered aviation broker within 10 business days of the acceleration notice. The two unencumbered G4-class aircraft are marketed for sale immediately.     Portfolio Company Equity — Ownership Transfer & Sale

    What happens: The Fund exercises its rights under the pledge agreements to take legal control of the Borrower's equity interests in all portfolio companies. Unlike most creditors who merely claim against assets, Draft Horse steps into ownership — giving it control over timing and sale process.

    The strategic advantage: If the portfolio companies are healthy and generating cash, the Fund can elect to operate them during the sale process, extracting ongoing distributions while marketing for a full exit. The Fund is not forced into a distressed fire sale — it controls the timeline.

    Real Property & Remaining Assets

    4. Principal balance — full return of invested capital
